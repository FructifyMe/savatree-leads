Executive Summary

Verdict: Feasible — with the right data tier.
Free satellite data (Sentinel-2, Landsat) can detect neighborhood-scale canopy decline but cannot pinpoint individual residential properties. For property-level lead generation in Jackson's six target towns, the most practical MVP combines free NAIP aerial imagery (0.6m resolution, updated every 3 years) with free Sentinel-2 NDVI time-series to flag areas of decline, then cross-references with property/parcel data to generate actionable leads.

The concept is sound and there's real science behind it: multispectral imaging (especially near-infrared) can detect tree stress 12–18 months before it's visible to the human eye. The question is resolution and cost. Here's what we found.

Detection Methods: What Actually Works

NDVI — The Core Technique

NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index) is the standard metric. It uses near-infrared and red light bands to measure vegetation health on a scale from -1 to +1. Healthy trees score 0.6–0.9; stressed or dead vegetation drops below 0.3. By comparing NDVI values across time (e.g., summer 2023 vs. summer 2025), you can identify where canopy has declined.

Your instinct about infrared was exactly right — NDVI relies on near-infrared reflectance, which is far more sensitive to plant health than visible-light photos. A tree can look green to the eye but already show declining NIR reflectance weeks before leaves start to yellow or thin.

ISA Tree Risk Assessment — How It Connects

The ISA (International Society of Arboriculture) uses a qualitative risk matrix with four levels: Low, Moderate, High, and Extreme. Their TRAQ (Tree Risk Assessment Qualification) system evaluates likelihood of failure × consequence of impact. There are three assessment levels:

  • Level 1 (Limited Visual): Rapid assessment from a distance — this is what satellite/aerial analysis could replicate at scale
  • Level 2 (Basic): Walk-around visual inspection — what Jackson would do on a property visit
  • Level 3 (Advanced): Detailed investigation with tools — the upsell service
Key insight: The ISA doesn't have a specific "canopy loss score" from remote sensing. However, satellite NDVI decline maps directly to a Level 1-style screening: identifying properties that warrant a Level 2 on-site assessment. This is the lead generation hook — "Our aerial analysis flagged potential tree health concerns on your property. We'd like to offer a complimentary Level 2 assessment."

MVP Pipeline — Proposed Architecture

1

Define Target Areas

Draw boundaries around the 6 towns using MassGIS parcel data. Filter to residential properties with lot sizes > 0.15 acres (likely to have significant tree coverage).

2

Build NDVI Baseline (Google Earth Engine)

Pull Sentinel-2 summer composites for the past 3–5 years. Calculate mean NDVI per parcel for each year. Flag parcels where NDVI dropped > 15% year-over-year — that's canopy loss. This is free using Google Earth Engine's Python API.

3

Visual Confirmation (NAIP)

For flagged parcels, pull 0.6m NAIP imagery to visually confirm the decline isn't just a mowed lawn or pool installation. This step can be partially automated with a simple tree canopy classifier (green pixels above a certain NDVI threshold in the NIR band).

4

Lead Scoring

Score each flagged property: severity of NDVI decline × number of large trees on lot × lot value (from assessor data). High-value properties with significant canopy loss = highest priority leads.

5

Property Owner Lookup

Cross-reference parcel IDs with MassGIS / town assessor databases to get owner names and mailing addresses. Massachusetts property records are public.

6

Outreach

Generate personalized mailers or door-hanger flyers: "Our satellite analysis detected changes in your property's tree canopy. We'd like to offer a free arborist consultation." Include a before/after thumbnail image of their specific property.

Risks & Limitations

  • Mixed pixel problem: At 10m resolution, a Sentinel-2 pixel on a residential lot mixes trees + house + lawn + driveway. NDVI decline could be a new roof, not a dead tree. NAIP confirmation step mitigates this.
  • NAIP update lag: NAIP is only updated every ~3 years. Recent canopy loss (last 12 months) may not appear. Sentinel-2 fills the gap for temporal detection, NAIP for spatial confirmation.
  • Cloud cover in New England: Sentinel-2 images are frequently cloudy. Must use cloud-masked composites across multiple dates per season.
  • False positives: Construction, landscaping changes, seasonal deciduous variation can trigger NDVI decline. Manual review of high-priority leads is essential before outreach.
  • Privacy and reception: "We analyzed your property from space" could feel invasive to some homeowners. Messaging should emphasize community tree health, not surveillance. Frame it as a neighborhood assessment program.

Recommended Next Steps

A

Proof of Concept — Pick One Town

Start with Wakefield or Reading (dense residential canopy). Pull 3 years of Sentinel-2 NDVI data via Google Earth Engine. Identify the top 20 parcels with largest NDVI decline. Manually verify with NAIP/Google Earth. If 10+ are legitimate tree health issues → the method works.

B

Build the Script

We can write a Python script that automates steps 1–5 for any town boundary. Output: a CSV of flagged addresses with decline scores, owner names, and thumbnail images.

C

Test the Outreach

Jackson sends 20–50 personalized mailers to flagged properties. Track response rate. If > 5% convert to consultations → scale to all 6 towns.

D

Scale with PlanetScope (if ROI justifies)

If the free-tier MVP generates real leads, upgrade to PlanetScope (3m, daily updates) for near-real-time detection. Could catch storm damage within days and trigger immediate outreach.

Interesting: Who Else Is Doing This?

ForSens / Karuna Technology (Germany, ESA spin-off) is the closest analog — they provide AI-powered satellite tree monitoring for cities like NYC, Zurich, and Rome. They detect vitality decline 16–18 months before visible symptoms. However, they sell to municipalities, not individual arborists. Jackson could potentially partner with or learn from their approach.

Purdue University's uTREE Project (funded $5M by NSF, Feb 2026) uses PlanetScope data + AI to map 278 million trees across 330 US cities. Their dataset could become a free resource as the project matures. Worth watching.

EarthDefine TreeMap provides high-resolution tree cover data commercially. Could be a shortcut if the DIY approach proves too labor-intensive.
